Query: What are some examples of community based COVID management centres?

 

Compiled by Meghendra Banerjee, PeerConsult Facilitator

Issue Date: 14 August 2020

 

 

From: Anirban Roy, Senior Domain Specialist, Supply Chain and Logistics, Kolkata

 

Posted 5 August 2020


My colony, a 300-house residential area in Kolkata, plans to set up a community-based COVID management centre where we can quarantine COVID-positive patients so they can receive proper treatment.  We will be refurbishing empty apartments and community centres into quarantine / treatment areas.

 

Kindly share details of similar initiatives in urban areas or suggestions on how to do this.

Summary of Responses

 

Seven respondents to this fast track query offered guidelines, including from the Government, and examples of nine community based COVID management centres across India.  One contributor offered a cautionary tale of a rejected initiative in Andhra Pradesh due to inadequate consultation with residents of the area.

 

Contributors also pointed out that self-isolation at home for asymptomatic and mildly symptomatic patients is a definite possibility; however, it could also create problems in the locality. The stigma associated with COVID is a big issue for those who are living now ‘in the midst of extreme anxiety, suppressed panic and anticipatory dread’.

 

The success of this programme will depend on sensitising the neighbours, reassuring them not to be afraid of contagion, and ensuring their support. Other than overall logistical preparedness, some important things to consider are developing appropriate linkages with the local government, care networks and treatment providers, not experimenting with drugs with unsubstantiated efficacy, and following strict hygiene and distancing practice. 

 

Comparative experiences and the resources recommended by contributors are presented below, followed by their responses in full.

 

 

Comparative Experiences

 

Bengaluru, Karnataka

 

Sobha City Covid Isolation Centre (from Dr. Deeksha Sharma, Nutrition Consultant, Writer & Natural Living Enthusiast, Bangalore)

Sobha City Apartment Owners Association created a sub-committee for Covid Care and set up a “Sobha City Covid Isolation Centre” (SCCIC) using the 8 Guest Rooms in the second floor of the Club House, meeting the requirements of Bruhat Bengaluru Mahanagara Palike (BBMP) guidelines and the medical service providers recommendations.

 

Private Operators Set Covid Care Centres in Gated Communities (from Meghendra Banerjee)

Private operators have already set up Covid Care Centres on the premises of Celestial Green, RMZ, Embassy group, Adarsh group, Mahaveer, and Purva apartments, all large gated communities with hundreds of families. Community-level care centres ensure the cost is shared and makes it affordable. It’s within reach day or night and reduces dependency on external resources. People with mild symptoms do not require hospitalisation; such infections can be managed effectively in such community centres.

 

Gurugram, Haryana

 

Community support to Covid patients in a township (from Shruti Ahuja, Independent Consultant, Gurugram, Haryana)

In a township in Gurugram, Haryana, the Covid patients were served food by delivery kitchens run by other residents. The security guards supported in the pick and drop of food without making any contact with the patients. They identified volunteers- doctors, a person who could help in delivering essential items and someone who could check on the patients on a regular basis.

 

Andhra Pradesh

 

Residents oppose quarantine facilities in residential areas (from Shruti Ahuja, Independent Consultant, Gurugram, Haryana)

The opening of quarantine facilities in residential areas and private colonies has drawn flak from the residents in a few areas. In one case protests started when a private college was being considered as a Covid care centre. In another area the authorities chose a new shopping complex constructed in the colony for setting up of the facility. the residents strongly opposed the idea and therefore the quarantine facility could not be created.

 

Kerala

 

Surge Preparedness by Govt. of Kerala (from EM Rafique, Cardio-Vascular Health Officer, (CVHO), WHO, Wayanad District, Kerala

The Kerala government’s strategy to deal with the expected COVID-19 surge is to bring those who are found positive for the COVID-19 test and have no symptoms to COVID-19 First Line Treatment Centres, which are hotels, wedding halls, schools, hostels, stadiums, and colleges identified by the Local Self Government bodies and set up as public health centres with essential facilities that can take up the extra surge load of cases.

 

Kolkata, West Bengal

Initiatives of Kolkata Housing Complexes (from Meghendra Banerjee)

 

South City Complex

South City Welfare association have planned to convert their club into a mini covid-care centre with 10 rooms acting as safe homes for patients who may need isolation. Discussion are in the final stages and they believe they will be able to prepare a full-fledged Medicare facility for the residents.

 

Upohar Complex

At Upohar Condominium, the board of managers held an emergency meeting on primarily to discuss the possibility of hosting a covid-care facility. In the absence of a separate community hall, they are planning to convert the ground floor activity centres. However, the centre doesn’t have a separate washroom. The discussion is currently on for solving this problem.

 

Silver Spring Complex

At places where community centres are too small, resident welfare association are thinking of few large empty flats converted into safe homes. However, it is still in the discussion stage.

 

Merlin Warden Lakeview

The residents’ association is planning to buy large quantities of equipment, like sphygmomanometer, pulse oximeter, oxygen cylinders, oxygen delivery tubes and face masks. Their community hall doesn’t have space to convert it into a mini covid-care unity, but they are stocking up to respond to future emergencies.

E. M. Rafique, Cardio-Vascular Health Officer, (CVHO), WHO, Wayanad District, Kerala

 

The Kerala government has come up with a strategy to specifically deal with the expected COVID-19 surge. Herein, those who are found positive for the COVID-19 test and have no symptoms will be taken to the COVID-19 First Line Treatment Centres (CFLTC) by designated ambulances, whose drivers are tagged by a mobile app.  The Local Self Government bodies had identified hotels, wedding halls, schools, hostels, stadiums, and colleges for setting up these first-line treatment centres.  Now, these CFLTCs are public health centres, with essential facilities that can take up the extra surge load of cases, by acting as a buffer, and thus will protect the functioning of the existing public health system in the event of a rapid community spread.

 

The Departments of Health, Local Self Government Department (LSGD) and the District's Disaster Management Authority are jointly setting up more treatment centres so that districts have around 9000 to 10000 extra beds on an average. Also, the small and medium private hospitals are allowed to run these CFLTCs.

 

Alongside, all Ambulances in the district are roped in as they are registered with the Regional Transport Office (RTO), and their details along with those of the CFLTCs are entered by a team of data entry operators into the COVID-19 dashboard called the COVID-19 Jagratha Portal. Thus, this portal which public can easily access, helps everyone to see in real time the details of beds available in any CFLTC in their districts, and the nearest ambulance, which can be summoned for transport to and from the CFLTC.

 

The data entry of CFLTC, the occupancy status of beds, the geo-location of the Ambulances, and updating these are the key to the success of this strategy.

 

 

Shruti Ahuja, Independent Consultant, Gurugram, Haryana

 

The idea of protecting family members while appropriate care is given to Covid patients is a very important consideration, more so in a setting like yours. Across the country many experiences of handling a similar situation are emerging.

 

To ensure its success, it would be critical for the residents of your colony to own this idea and support it till the end. Here’s a story from Maddilapalem, Andhra Pradesh where the residents strongly opposed the idea and therefore the quarantine facility could not be created. Read here - "Plan to open quarantine facility in residential area opposed" - https://www.thehindu.com/news/cities/Visakhapatnam/plan-to-open-quarantine-facility-in-residential-area-opposed/article31512769.ece   

 

So, I strongly feel consensus building is needed before you get into this.

 

Once that is done it will be important to raise volunteers- doctors, a person who could help in delivering essential items and someone who could check on the patients on a regular basis. Apart from these garbage collection and food are two important areas that need attention.  In our township in Gurugram, Haryana, the Covid patients were served food by delivery kitchens run by other residents. The security guards supported in the pick and drop of food without making any contact with the patients.

 

Finally, please refer to the Guidelines from National Centre for Disease Control (NCDC) at https://ncdc.gov.in/index1.php?lang=1&level=1&sublinkid=703&lid=550 for specific requirements - Quarantine Facilities, Home Quarantine, Waste Management and Home Based Care.

 

Hope these are useful and best of luck.

Greetings in the time of corona! At a rather fringe area of West Bengal, Raiganj, we have organized a supplementary health consultation facility to help people who are living now in the midst of extreme anxiety, suppressed panic and anticipatory dread. My mobile number is - 7001910020.

 

What I feel can be put point wise -

 

(1) as 80-81% cases are asymptomatic, pauci-symptomatic or mild to moderate symptoms, they should be first reassured not to be afraid of death,

 

(2) as the latest research paper from JAMA (South Korean study) shows the amount of viral shedding is tantamount to symptomatic patients so they should be strictly asked to follow good face mask (even in home), frequent practice of hand hygiene and self-distancing of 8 feet (as airborne transmission, which is to an extent, is a paradigmatic shift in our perception of transmission),

 

(3) to AVOID unnecessary and useless drugs (which have been discarded in repeated double-blind, placebo controlled, RCTs, like HCQS and ivermectin), and

 

(4) to take drugs as little as possible.

 

Finally, attending physicians and health workers are expected to be conversant with the latest research papers and updated guidelines.

 

Wishing everyone a smiling life.
